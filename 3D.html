3D Animation and Modeling

The first two forms of animation that I did were 2D hand-drawn animation and stop-motion animation. But after discovering 2D animation, I realized that I needed a PC to animate with, because my old laptop was not going to cut it. So I bought the needed parts and assembled a powerful workstation. While this was going on, my brother became interested in 3D modeling, something that I knew nothing about. I became interested, and realized that there were only two ingredients needed to create 3D: a powerful computer, and software. I had just bought a powerful computer, and my brother told me about this free 3D modeling software: Blender. I downloaded and opened Blender, not knowing a thing about it, and was instantly lost. So I asked my brother and he recommended this guy on Youtube, Blender Guru(Andrew Price), who could teach me how to use Blender. Like millions before me I did Blender Guru's Blender 2.8 Beginner tutorial series(now updated, see Blender Guru's 3.0 Beginner tutorial series), which was 23 15ish minute lessons on how to model a doughnut and a cup of coffee. It took weeks of hard work to get it, but at the end I could use Blender, and make a doughnut. I first began 3D in May 2021, and I have improved alot since then. Now I can make a decent doughnut in about 5 minutes. Here is my 3D portfolio.
The Blender 2.8 Beginner tutorial series doughnut: my first official doughnut render. This is what I spent 2 months learning how to do.
After completing the beginner series, I expanded my horizon to other things, discovering such people as Ian Hubert and Steve from CG Geek along the way, who through their tutorial videos taught me more advanced Blender methods. So while doughnuts are the best, I do in fact have other 3D skills. Here are some other miscellaneous works.
Beach Waves: using Blender's built in fluid simulator, Mantaflow, I created this beach wave effect with some rocks and sand.
Exploding Planet: using rigid body simulations and the cell fracture addon I was able to explode a planet. I learned how to do this in a tutorial by CG Geek.
Exploding Building: Using essentially the same techniques as the exploding planet I wa sable to destroy this building. Using rigid body simulations, the cell fracture addon and a smoke simulation I was able to destroy this building.
Underwater Reef: if you get the volumetrics right, you can make almost anything look like it is under water. I make a plane, gave it a sand material and put some rocks on it. I then made a cube, covered the whole thing and gave the cube a volume shader that I tweaked until it looked right. After that I added some low resolution icospheres and sticks being emitted from above to form water particles and junk. The fish were made by projecting a fish picture onto a fish shaped mesh, animating it going straight forward, and then using a displacement modifier with a clouds texture set to global coordinates. This made the fish wiggle like it was swimming. Then I duplicated the one fish, deleted the duplicate's animation data and parented it to the animated fish. I then just duplicated and scaled until I was happy. I learned many of the techniques from a video by Kev from KevBinge, and the fish were inspired by an Ian Hubert video .
Yo Obi-Wan-Hologram: A spoof on Star Wars. I used the techniques learned from a Blender Guru tutorial(this video has audio if you are interested).
Dynamic Snow: A CG Geek tutorial taught me how to make snow pile up on the surface of an object. In this simple demonstration one can see snow falling onto the ground and a tree, as well as a ball displacing to snow as it rolls into view.
Wood Samples: A render I did for my employer Solid Custom Heilooms that illustrates some of our wood sample stain choices. It is one of if not the most photo-realistic works I have done so far. I made it by animating some rectangles and putting the images of our wood samples on them, a different stain for either side. This proved to be a more difficult task then one might think, as putting two image textures on one object and separating them by a set divide in a natural way is tricky to do in Blender, at least so far as I could tell. I ended up using a gradient texture and color-ramp node to achieve this effect.
LEGO Bricks: A bunch of computer generated LEGO bricks. The brick model was exported from the LEGO modeling application Stud.io and imported into Blender. In Blender the model was duplicated and made into a pile using a rigid body simulation.

VFX

VFX (Visual Effects) Is the art of using special effects(usually but not always by a computer) to enhance real life. Whether it is an extreme explosion dust particles no one notices, the purpose of VFX is to do what real life means cannot.
Putting a Kitchen into a City Appartment: I took a video of a kitchen, then modeled a mesh that looked like the footage, and if you line everything up correctly you can put live action footage into a 3D scene. I learned this from Ian Hubert.
Drone on Desk: I took a video of a desk, motion tracked the footage in Blender, and took 6 additional pictures of the surronding environment. I configured these 6 pictures into a box formation around my 3D subject, and made that box emit light. I modeled a droid and animated it doing droid stuff in the midst of that box. The lighting provided a sense that the droid was in the space. I added a plane to catch the shadows and there it is.
